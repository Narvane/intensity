# How It Works

This document explains the general dynamics of the Intensity experience — how users interact with the product, the main flows they perceive, and the core concepts in everyday language. It does not describe technical implementation or detailed operational rules.

**Audience:** users, stakeholders, and anyone who wants to understand the product experience without technical or functional specification detail.

---

## Short

Intensity works in two complementary rhythms. **Alone**, each person registers experience ideas into shared **boxes** tied to a **group**. **Together**, the group enters the **Experience Box**, draws one idea at random, sees its intensity first, aligns as a group, and then **reveals** the full experience. Ideas are collected over time; memorable moments happen when everyone is present.

---

## Medium

### First contact

On first launch, users see a short **onboarding** in four steps: the problem (repetitive experiences, lack of closeness), the insight (memorable moments are usually unexpected but get postponed), the call to action (do not wait — Intensity pushes you to act), and the mechanism (collect unusual ideas, draw one, live memorable moments). A **quick guide** is available from onboarding or the login screen.

### Two ways to enter

The app offers two entry modes that reflect two moments in the same journey:

| Mode (as shown to users) | Who enters | What they do |
|--------------------------|------------|--------------|
| **Experiences** | One person (the "proposer") | Register experiences and choose which group and box to add them to |
| **Experience Box** | The whole group, together | Create boxes, browse them, and live the draw-and-reveal moment |

A **group** is formed automatically by the unique combination of people who log in together in Experience Box mode. The same people may belong to different groups depending on who joins each session.

### The collect phase (over time)

When contributing alone through **Experiences**, the user picks a group, then a box within that group, and sees the experiences they registered in that session. They can **create a new experience** through a guided flow with five conceptual stages:

1. **Suggestion** — describe an idea or tap an example for inspiration
2. **Reflection** — consider whether everyone would accept the idea, however unusual, and explain why
3. **Parameters** — express how demanding, open, and novel the experience feels
4. **Classification** — assign an overall intensity level to the idea
5. **Bifurcation** — think about variations (place, context, duration) before saving; optionally save and create another

Boxes themselves are created in **Experience Box** mode, not during individual contribution. Each box has a **name** and a **type** that sets the theme — for example outings as a couple, trips with friends, breaking routine, first times, light discomfort, connection moments, or different experiences.

### The live moment (together)

When the group enters **Experience Box**, they see their boxes and can open one. Inside, they **draw an experience**. The draw can consider the full box or focus on a chosen intensity level. Once an experience is selected, the card first shows its **intensity and parameters** — not the full text. The interface prompts the group to **align before revealing**: agree on mood, limits, and commitment; consider whether they want something lighter first. Only after that alignment does the group **flip the card** to read the full experience. They can return to the draw to pick again.

### Core concepts users encounter

| Concept | What it means to the user |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **Experience** | A concrete idea to do together, with a description and a sense of how intense it is |
| **Box** | A themed container where experiences for a group are collected |
| **Group** | The set of people who entered Experience Box mode together |
| **Intensity** | How bold or demanding an experience feels overall |
| **Draw** | Random selection from the box — meant to be taken seriously |
| **Reveal** | The deliberate moment of flipping the card to see the full description |
| **Seal** | An integrity mark on the experience card |

### Recommended social rhythm

The quick guide describes a rhythm the product encourages but does not enforce: each person feeds the box over time; when everyone is together, they trigger the draw; before revealing, they align on mood, limits, and commitment.

## Detailed

### The experience arc from empty to lived

Intensity organizes the user journey around a cycle that spans days or weeks of contribution and a concentrated moment of togetherness:

```
Contribute ideas (alone)  →  Gather as a group  →  Draw  →  Align  →  Reveal  →  Live the moment
```

This cycle can repeat within the same box as new experiences are added and new draw moments happen.

### Onboarding as product narrative

The four onboarding steps are not merely instructional — they tell a story that mirrors the product logic:

1. **Recognition** — you feel the pain of repetitive, shallow experiences and miss real closeness
2. **Insight** — the moments you remember were unusual, yet you keep postponing them
3. **Agency** — waiting for the perfect opportunity is the trap; Intensity exists to push you to act
4. **Mechanism** — the answer is simple: collect unusual ideas, draw one, live memorable moments

This narrative reappears in the quick guide and shapes how the product expects users to think about their relationship with the app.

### Why two entry modes exist

The split between **Experiences** and **Experience Box** reflects a deliberate separation of contexts:

- **Contribution** happens individually, at each person's pace, without requiring the whole group to be present
- **Consumption** (draw and reveal) happens collectively, when emotional alignment matters most

A person who only ever enters through Experiences can feed boxes but cannot create them or trigger a group draw. Someone who enters through Experience Box defines the active group for that session and accesses the shared ritual.

### Groups and boxes

A **group** is not manually created or named by users — it emerges from who logs in together. This means the same person may appear in multiple groups depending on different combinations of friends or partners.

Within a group, **boxes** segment experiences by theme and occasion. Eleven box types were identified, covering social outings, travel, intimacy, routine-breaking, novelty, discomfort, connection, and uncommon experiences — for both couples and friend circles. Each type carries a short subtitle that hints at the kind of ideas expected (e.g., "light to intense group hangouts" or "step a bit out of comfort zone with care").

### Creating an experience — what the user goes through

The five-stage creation flow guides the proposer from a raw idea to a considered contribution:

**Suggestion** lowers the barrier to entry. Users who lack ideas can tap themed examples that populate the description field — editable at any stage.

**Reflection** asks a respectful question: would everyone accept this experience, however peculiar? The proposer must justify, helping the group connect with care rather than surprise.

**Parameters** invite the proposer to characterize the experience along three dimensions visible to the group later: how much **effort or commitment** it requires, how much **openness** it asks for, and how **novel** it is compared to what the group usually does.

**Classification** consolidates those dimensions into a single **intensity** level — from light to adrenaline — that the group will see before the full text is revealed.

**Bifurcation** encourages creative variation before saving: small changes in place, context, duration, or audience can spawn new ideas. The user can save and immediately start another, building a batch of related experiences.

The interface also states that **experiences are not encrypted** — a transparency notice to the user about how their text is handled.

### The draw-and-reveal ritual

The shared moment is the emotional center of Intensity. When a box has experiences, the group can **activate** a draw. The product supports choosing among different intensity scopes — any experience, an exact intensity, or up to a maximum intensity — so the group can match the moment's mood.

The selected experience appears as a **card**. Initially, only intensity and parameters are visible. A dashed prompt reminds the group to **decide before revealing** — to agree whether they want something lighter, to use filters if needed, and to align on commitment.

Flipping the card is a deliberate gesture: **reveal** the full experience text. Additional information from the creation flow (such as the proposer's justification) can be shown for transparency. A **seal** on the card represents integrity of the content.

If the draw does not fit the moment, the group can go **back to draw** and try again.

### What users see vs. what the guide recommends

The app implements the collect-draw-reveal loop and the creation wizard. The quick guide additionally recommends practices that exist **only as guidance**, not as app features:

- Defining a **consequence** (a real cost if someone backs out) before revealing
- **Swapping** experiences of different intensity levels (e.g., trading one high-intensity draw for two lower ones)
- **Gradual progression** through intensity levels over time

These social rules enrich the experience but are not enforced or tracked by the application.

### Language and accessibility

Users can switch the interface among Portuguese, English, and Italian. The product brand remains "Intensity" across languages. Example suggestions in the creation flow appear in Portuguese regardless of the selected interface language.

## Gaps and limitations

| Topic | Status |
|-------|--------|
| Consequence definition and tracking | Described in the quick guide only — no interface found |
| Intensity swap rules | Described in the quick guide only — not implemented in the app |
| Full reflection questionnaire | Additional reflection fields exist in data structures but only one question is asked in the creation flow |
| Suggestion examples in English and Italian | Examples appear in Portuguese only |
| Enforcement of the recommended social rhythm | The guide describes a flow; the app does not block or remind users to follow it step by step |
