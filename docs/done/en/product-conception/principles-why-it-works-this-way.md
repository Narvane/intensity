# Principles — Why It Works This Way

This document captures the philosophy, objectives, and conceptual premises behind Intensity — why the product is designed the way it is, and which problems it seeks to solve. It is based on the product's guiding principles and observable design choices, not on developer intent.

**Audience:** users, stakeholders, managers, investors, and product analysts — no technical knowledge required.

---

## Short

Intensity exists because memorable connection rarely happens by accident — unusual experiences get postponed, and routines fill the space. The product replaces passive waiting with a simple structure: collect ideas over time, take a random draw seriously when together, and decide as a group before revealing. Its principles are **connection, intensity, and discovery** — lived with presence, not as tasks to complete.

---

## Medium

### Problems the product addresses

Intensity responds to three intertwined frustrations:

1. **Repetitive experiences** that do not deepen relationships with the people who matter
2. **Recognizable value in the unusual** — people know their best moments were often unexpected, yet they keep deferring them
3. **The trap of waiting** — holding out for the perfect opportunity instead of creating conditions to act

The product does not try to generate ideas from nothing. It assumes people already have impulses toward unusual experiences but lack a **structure and a moment** to act on them.

### Guiding philosophy

The product essence is:

- **Connection, intensity, and discovery**
- **Not about completing a task** — about living meaningful moments with **presence**

Every major design choice serves this philosophy: the draw is random but serious; the reveal is delayed until the group aligns; contribution happens calmly over time, not under pressure.

### Core premises

| Premise | How it shows up |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Do not leave connection to chance** | Collect ideas proactively; use the draw as a commitment trigger |
| **Take the draw seriously** | Randomness is a decision tool, not entertainment |
| **Decide before revealing** | Show intensity first; align mood, limits, and commitment; then flip the card |
| **Respect the group** | Reflection before adding an idea; justification visible for transparency |
| **Grow gradually** | Start with lighter experiences; evolve intensity over time |
| **Accountability matters** | Define consequences with real cost before revealing |

### Conceptual trade-offs

Intensity makes deliberate choices that shape the experience:

**Asynchronous contribution, synchronous revelation.** Ideas are added alone, at each person's pace. The draw and reveal require the group to be together. This respects different schedules while preserving the emotional weight of the shared moment.

**Secrecy until alignment.** The full experience text is hidden until the group has seen intensity, discussed limits, and chosen to reveal. This prevents surprise from overriding consent.

**Individual curation, collective consumption.** Two entry modes instead of one unified flow — because proposing an idea and living a draw are different psychological contexts.

**Structured randomness.** The draw is random, but the group can scope it by intensity level. Randomness proposes; the group disposes.

**Voluntary adoption.** Consequences, swaps, and gradual intensity progression enrich the experience when the group chooses to embrace them — the product trusts each group to find its own rhythm.

### What Intensity is not

Understanding the principles also means recognizing boundaries:

- It is **not a task manager** or habit tracker
- It is **not a social network** for sharing with strangers
- It is **not a content platform** with curated experiences from the product itself — all ideas come from the group
- It is **not a game of chance** — take the draw seriously; do not treat it lightly

## Detailed

### From problem to product logic

The onboarding narrative builds a causal chain that justifies every feature at a conceptual level:

```
Repetitive experiences
    → lack of closeness
        → memorable moments were the unusual ones
            → but those get postponed
                → waiting replaces acting
                    → Intensity: collect, draw, live
```

This is not marketing fluff — it is the **reasoning backbone** of the product. Each step explains why the next design element exists. Collection addresses postponement. Drawing addresses decision paralysis. Reveal-with-alignment addresses consent and emotional readiness. Intensity levels address the need to match experience boldness to the group's current mood.

### The principle of deliberate randomness

Many products use randomness for gamification. Intensity inverts that expectation: the central rule is to **take the draw seriously**. Randomness here functions as a **fairness and commitment mechanism** — when the group agrees to draw, the result carries weight. Nobody chose it unilaterally; everyone accepted the process.

This principle explains why the product shows intensity and parameters **before** the full description: the group commits to the *level* of experience before knowing the *specific* idea. They can accept, re-draw with a lighter filter, or pause — but they do not get ambushed by content.

### The principle of presence over completion

The essence statement rejects task-completion framing explicitly. Intensity does not track streaks, award points, or mark experiences as "done." An experience is an **invitation to a moment**, not an item on a checklist.

This principle permeates the language: "live memorable moments," "presence," "connection," "align mood." Even the creation wizard's reflection step asks whether the group would **accept** an idea — not whether they will **finish** it.

### The principle of respectful reflection

Before an idea enters the shared box, the proposer must reflect on group acceptance. The question — would everyone agree to this experience, however unusual? — embeds **empathy and consent** into the contribution act itself.

This is not a veto system (the group sees the result only at draw time), but it plants a norm: ideas are offered with care, not as pranks or tests. Additional information from the creation flow can be revealed on the card for transparency if disputes arise.

### The principle of gradual intensity

Start with lower intensity levels and evolve gradually. Use swaps between intensity levels and filters during the draw when the moment calls for it. These practices acknowledge that **trust and courage build over time** — a group that jumps straight to the boldest experiences may break the social contract the product depends on.

Intensity provides intensity filters and levels; progression and swap rules remain choices the group makes together.

### The principle of social accountability

Define **consequences** before revealing — a real cost agreed upon by the group, scaled if someone backs out, and adjusted when swaps occur. Shared experiences need **social contracts** that go beyond the app itself. Groups that embrace this practice strengthen the draw's seriousness; those that prefer a lighter approach rely on trust alone.

### The principle of group sovereignty

"If in doubt, align as a group before revealing" — the product's closing tip summarizes a recurring theme: **the group decides**. Filters, re-draws, pauses, and reveal timing are all group choices. Intensity provides structure and information; it does not dictate outcomes.

This explains the Experience Box entry mode requiring all members to log in together: the active group for a session is whoever is present, and their combination defines the social unit.

### Conceptual trade-offs in depth

**Two modes instead of one.** A single unified app could let anyone create boxes and draw at any time. Intensity separates proposer and group contexts. Trade-off: slightly more complexity at login. Benefit: contribution without social pressure; revelation with full group presence.

**Random draw vs. free choice.** The group could simply pick an experience from a list. Randomness removes negotiation deadlocks and spreads authorship — everyone's ideas have equal chance. Trade-off: less control. Benefit: surprise, fairness, and commitment.

**Hidden text until reveal vs. full transparency.** Showing everything upfront would be simpler. Delayed reveal creates a ritual moment and forces intensity alignment first. Trade-off: friction. Benefit: consent, anticipation, and shared decision.

**Guidance over rigid rules.** Consequences, swaps, and progression are recommended social practices, not imposed requirements. Trade-off: groups may adapt or skip them. Benefit: flexibility for different relationship dynamics and maturity levels.

### Objectives the product pursues

Synthesizing the evidence, Intensity aims to:

1. **Reduce postponement** of meaningful shared experiences
2. **Increase unusual and connecting moments** within existing relationships
3. **Provide structure** without prescribing specific activities
4. **Balance surprise with consent** through the draw-and-reveal ritual
5. **Encourage presence** in the moment of living an experience
6. **Build group norms** of respect, gradual courage, and accountability
